2.671 Measurement & Instrumentation

The Physics of a Cheerleading Back Handspring

In my freshman and sophomore years, I joined the MIT Cheerleading Team on a whim after a close friend pressured me to go to a couple practices. Even though I hadn't done any organized athletics since middle school (my high school was a charter school and didn't have sports), the team was really welcoming and our coaches walked us through everything. We were able to hit stunts I never imagined I'd be a part of. The one hitch? It's really hard to pick up tumbling as an adult. Telling yourself to throw your body backwards and around when there's so much of you to throw is VERY frightening. But it can also be a difficult mental game to move from the spring floor we learn on to the competition floor, and to connect a trick to a roundoff, which is meant to give you and your tumbling even more momentum.

For 2.671, a class designed to teach students how to ask questions and go about quantitatively finding an answer, I decided to investigate the differences between a standing back handspring and a running back handspring. I also looked at the difference between a back handspring on mat floor and spring floor. I wanted to be able to tell my teammates who had gotten their standing back handspring on the spring floor (which is what everyone starts learning with) that they could 1. definitely get it on the mat floor if they put their mind to it, and 2. that even though it can be tricky to figure out how to connect a roundoff into a back handspring, everything would be so much easier in the end.

While it is fairly intuitive that starting from a roundoff means you don't need as much pushing force in your arms to gain momentum, it was interesting to see that switching from the spring floor to competition mat floor was not actually significantly more difficult. I also expected Jasmin's body form to change between each of the four conditions to compensate for changes in her input momentum or the spring floor's extra cushion, but the key body parameters hardly actually changed, if at all, suggesting that there's no real trick to getting a back handspring under one set of conditions compared to another.
