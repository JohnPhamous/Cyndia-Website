2.72 Elements of Mechanical Design

Desktop Lathe

Over the course of a semester, my team modelled, designed, fabricated, and characterized a desktop lathe that reached 10-15 micron tolerances in diameter, tape, and runout/eccentricity. I took on the role of FEA Guru and Modelling Deputy Guru to make sure that structural elements were as stiff as necessary to reach our desired cutting tolerances and that our tool translation system was accurate.

We were given some stock, an example CAD model (full of errors, but to give us an idea of size) and a goal of 50 micron tolerance. We had to determine the functional requirements of the system - material removal rate, efficiency, etc. - and refine the design based on our error model calculations. Then we had to create our own CAD model and machining process plans to produce our parts on the waterjet and lathe, or whatever else necessary (including a 3D printer to print our glow-in-the-dark pulleys). In the class's final "death test," the lathe also had to survive being dropped from waist height and the carriage still needed to translate even with a person standing on it.

We each had Guru and Deputy Guru positions to make sure someone was holding down the fort in each of the areas of CAD, Modelling, Machining, Texting/Experimentation, and FEA. As Modelling Deputy Guru, I helped develop a MATLAB model of errors & deflections in each component of the lathe using Homogeneous Transformation Matrices (HTMs). We followed the load path from the tool and from the chuck down to the base, also accounting for thermal effects. The model helped us confirm what approximate dimensions we needed to reach our tolerance goal.

As FEA Guru, I optimized our lathe's flexures. The crossfeed flexure gives the tool x-axis freedom, so it was designed for approximately a 0.6" range of motion - the chuck fits parts up to 0.5". In order to achieve linear motion, the carriage is separated from the tool by two stages of flexures (called a serial flexure due to the existence of an intermediate stage). While thinner flexures would allow for more range before yield of the aluminum, we also had to consider the capabilities of the waterjet to create such thin features. For example, since the waterjet nozzle slows down on turns, we had to increase the fillet radius on the connections between each flexure and base to make sure the joint wasn't completely eaten away.

Below is the dancing man flexure. He absorbs expected cutting forces (left figure) and assembly misalignment/component errors (right figure) to allow translation only in the z direction without binding. This required playing with relative stiffnesses in each component of the assembly. The dancing man was designed to be about five times more compliant in every degree of freedom other than the z direction, while he was as stiff as possible in the z direction given material and machining constraints. Due to the nature of a 5 DOF flexure (you can read more about them here), two different sets of serial flexures had to be designed (the center and the end plates) and assembled in parallel.
